I believe that the modern researcher views the data sequence as something 'really' processed by linear means, the parts that are streamlined for efficiency appearing much brighter than those left to the old overhead. It follows that if we apply a sparse pattern or a low-rank approximation, no two layers will see exactly the same distribution of dependencies, because no two can view the sequence from exactly the same functional point of view, and any change in the architecture makes some change in the way the data is reflected. For most practical applications these approximations are unimportant, but to the architect they are all-important: the designer has to unlearn the habit of thinking that the model 'really' sees the whole, and to learn the habit of seeing information as it appears through a sliding window. Here we have the beginning of a distinction between 'full' attention and 'efficient' representation—between what the sequence seems to be and what the model calculates it to be. It is evident from what we have found, that there is no kernel or memory module which pre-eminently appears to be the 'true' state of the data; it appears differently to a kernel-based method than it does to an adaptive span, and there is no reason for regarding one of these as more really the context than the other.
