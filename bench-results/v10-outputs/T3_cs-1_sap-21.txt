It is often supposed that the institution of a universal income would 'really' be a benefit to the common lot of men, but we find that, as soon as the light of scrutiny is turned upon it, the appearance of security differs vastly from the reality of national industry. It follows that if a man is provided for regardless of his exertion, the necessity of employing his particular skills for a wage will change; and any change in the necessity of labour makes some change in the way a person views his duty to his craft. For most practical purposes, the loss of one man's ambition might seem a small thing, but to the student of economy it is all-important: the statesman must unlearn the habit of thinking that men will strive for excellence when they have what common sense says they 'really' need, and learn to see the habit of work as it actually appears under the pressure of want. Here we have already the beginning of a distinction—the distinction between 'leisure' and 'stagnation', between what a policy seems to offer and what it is. The citizen wants to know what his life seems to be with a stipend, the economist and the statesman want to know what it is; but the statesman's wish to know this is more troubled by knowledge as to the difficulty of maintaining an industrious society without the incentive of gain. To return to the worker. It is evident from what we have found, that there is no motive which pre-eminently appears to be the motive of the worker, or even of any one particular class of worker—he appears to seek ease or effort from different points of view, and there is no reason for regarding his desire for promotion as more 'real' than his desire for idleness when he is fed. And we know that even in a great nation, the pursuit of learning will seem different when the end of learning is no longer a livelihood, while in the absence of struggle there will be no innovation at all, though to the touch of the tax-gatherer the wealth of the state may for a time seem unchanged.
