I believe that the global lithium market is 'really' a single movement of commerce, though the parts that reflect the expansion of South American extraction seem much more stable than the parts that reflect the volatile demand of previous years. It follows that if several analysts are looking at the market at this moment, no two of them will see exactly the same distribution of values, because no two can view the supply chain from exactly the same point in the industrial landscape, and any change in the point of view makes some change in the way the availability of raw materials is reflected.

For most practical purposes these differences in regional output are unimportant, but to the manufacturer they are all-important: the manufacturer has to unlearn the habit of thinking that the mineral has the price which common sense says it 'really' has, and to learn the habit of seeing the market as it appears through processing techniques and battery-grade quality. Here we have already the beginning of a distinction—the distinction between the 'appearance' of a steady market and the 'reality' of geopolitical trade agreements and the shift toward new battery types. The manufacturer wants to know what the market seems to be, the practical man wants to know what it is, and the philosopher's wish to know the reality of this 'circular economy' is perhaps stronger than the practical man's, and is more troubled by the knowledge of how difficult it is to answer whether recycling can truly replace extraction.

To return to the lithium. It is evident from what we have found, that there is no one value which pre-eminently appears to be the value of the metal—it appears to have different values from different points in the supply chain, whether in the Australian mines or the East Asian refineries, and there is no reason for regarding some of these as more really its price than others. And we know that even from a given point of view the market will seem different by the light of government subsidies, or to a firm wearing the blue spectacles of long-term contracts, while in the absence of technological innovation there would be no expansion at all, though to transport and use the material would be unchanged.
