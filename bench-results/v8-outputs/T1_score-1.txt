We suppose that the modern researcher 'really' desires to process the whole of a sequence at once, yet the parts that reflect the computational light look much brighter when they are simplified, and some parts look obscure because of the weight of the data. It follows that if several methods are looking at the sequence at the same moment, no two of them will see exactly the same distribution of importance, because no two can see it from exactly the same structural point of view—the linear mechanism perceives a different reality from the sparse pattern. For most practical purposes these differences are unimportant, but to the engineer they are all-important: the engineer has to unlearn the habit of thinking that machines see the data which common sense says they 'really' see, and to learn the habit of seeing things as they appear through low-rank approximations or sliding windows. Here we have already the beginning of one of the distinctions that cause most trouble in research—the distinction between 'approximation' and 'fidelity', between what the data seem to be and what they are. To return to attention. It is evident from what we have found, that there is no single kernel or adaptive span which pre-eminently appears to be the span of the data—it appears to be of different breadths from different points of view, and there is no reason for regarding some of these as more really its meaning than others, while in the absence of specialized memory modules there would be no retention at all, though to the raw input the sequence remains unchanged.
