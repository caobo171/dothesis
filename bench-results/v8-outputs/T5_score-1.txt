I believe that the lithium market is 'really' a site of equilibrium in the coming year, though the parts that reflect the light of new extraction look much more stable than the parts still obscured by the shadows of past volatility. I know that, if the observer shifts his gaze towards the South American facilities, the apparent distribution of availability will change, for no two analysts can see the supply chain from exactly the same point of view. It follows that there is no single price which pre-eminently appears to be the value of the metal, but rather a series of appearances depending upon the point of view of the manufacturer and the reflection of industrial demand.

For most practical purposes these distinctions are unimportant, but to the industrialist they are all-important: the maker of batteries has to unlearn the habit of thinking that lithium has the availability which common sense says it 'really' has, and to learn the habit of seeing the resource as it appears through the lens of regulation and refining. Here we have the beginning of a distinction between 'raw' and 'refined', between what the earth offers and what the laboratory requires. The miner wants to know where the brine lies, but the strategist and the philosopher of commerce want to know what it will become; and the philosopher's wish is more troubled by knowledge as to the difficulties of ensuring a steady supply.

To return to the substance itself. It is evident from what we have found, that there is no method of extraction which is pre-eminently the true source—it appears to be recovered from the earth in some instances and from the scrap-heap in others, and there is no reason for regarding one as more really lithium than the other. And we know that even from a given point of view the market will seem different by the light of new technology, or to a man blinded by geopolitical spectacles, while in the dark of a trade embargo there will be no market at all, though to the touch of the engineer the metal will be unchanged.
