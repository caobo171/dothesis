It is widely supposed that the institution of a universal income would merely alter the mechanism of our economy, yet it seems more likely that it would unsettle the very foundation upon which our collective labour rests. For most practical purposes, the need for a wage is the chief impulse to activity; but if this impulse is removed, the habit of industry may be lost, and the competitive spirit which leads a man to employ his peculiar talents may give way to a general indifference. It follows that, if the reward for effort is no longer distinct from the reward for idleness, the student will find little reason to endure the hardships of study, and the worker will feel no compulsion to contribute to the national output. Here we encounter a difficulty of the first order—the distinction between a life of earned achievement and one of subsidized repose. To the leader of industry this change is all-important, for it removes the traditional incentives by which great enterprises are managed; and to the small merchant the burden of the necessary taxation might prove fatal to innovation. If the most capable among us are led by these circumstances to prefer ease to responsibility, the resilience of the private sector must inevitably decline, and the excellence of our services will fade as the participation of the many ceases to be a necessity of life.
