I have come to believe that the morning hours are 'really' of a singular potential, though the parts which we occupy with deliberate action look much more vital than the parts which we leave to chance. I know that, if I employ a mechanical clock to fix the moment of waking, the subsequent distribution of my energies will change; it follows that, by the application of water and the movement of the limbs, the body is brought into a state of readiness which the mind alone cannot command. For most practical purposes these small acts of preparation are unremarkable, but to the disciplined observer they are all-important: one must unlearn the habit of drifting into the day as it happens to arrive, and learn the habit of meeting it as a structured whole. Here we have already the beginning of a distinction that causes much difficulty in the conduct of life—the distinction between the 'busy' man and the 'effective' man, between the appearance of activity and the reality of achievement. To return to the planner and the meditation. It is evident from what I have found, that there is no single task which pre-eminently defines the day's success; but, by the quiet ordering of one's thoughts and the careful preparation of sustenance, a calm state of mind is achieved which would otherwise be lost in the fatigue of the afternoon. In the end, it is not merely the output that is increased, but the very clearness of the growth we seek to attain.
